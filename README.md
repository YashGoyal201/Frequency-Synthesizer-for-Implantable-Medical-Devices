Implantable medical devices (IMDs) are electronic systems embedded within the human body to support medical diagnostics and health monitoring. These diagnostics often require continuous tracking of physiological parameters such as blood pressure, body temperature, and
electrocardiogram signals. There is a growing demand in the market for IMDs that offer high performance while consuming ultra-low power. One example is the wireless capsule endoscope (WCE), a bio-monitoring IMD that transmits visual data from the gastrointestinal tract to an
external device using wireless technology. Another example is the retinal prosthesis, which helps restore visual perception by stimulating the eye. Efficient wireless communication between IMDs and external monitoring systems relies heavily on a reliable transceiver. In IMDs, the transceiver typically operates around 400 MHz frequency. A crucial element of this transceiver system is the frequency synthesizer. Since IMDs operate with limited power sources, to meet IMD requirements, the frequency synthesizer is designed to occupy minimal chip space, deliver high energy efficiency, and maintain excellent spectral purity in the generated clock signals. Phase-locked loop (PLL)-based frequency synthesizers are commonly employed for generating  on-chip clock signals in implantable medical devices (IMDs). A PLL operates as a negative feedback system, ensuring that the output frequency closely follows a stable reference signal provided by a crystal oscillator. The core components of a PLL include a phase-frequency detector (PFD), a loop filter, and a voltage-controlled oscillator (VCO). Among these, the VCO plays a key role in determining the overall performance of the PLL. Critical system characteristics such as oscillation frequency, tuning range, phase noise, and power consumption are largely influenced by the VCO’s design.
